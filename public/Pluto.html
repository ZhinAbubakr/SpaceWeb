Pluto

Pluto is a dwarf planet located in the Kuiper Belt, a region of icy objects beyond Neptune. It was discovered in 1930 and was considered the ninth planet in our solar system until 2006, when it was reclassified as a dwarf planet. Pluto has a diameter of approximately 1,473 miles (2,374 kilometers) and is composed primarily of rock and ice. It has a thin atmosphere of nitrogen, methane, and carbon monoxide that freezes and falls to the surface as the planet moves away from the Sun in its highly elliptical orbit. Pluto has five known moons, the largest of which is Charon. Charon is so large relative to Pluto that the two objects are often considered a binary system. Pluto's other moons are smaller and were only discovered in the years after Charon. Despite its small size, Pluto is a subject of scientific interest due to its unique characteristics and location in the Kuiper Belt. In 2015, the New Horizons spacecraft became the first spacecraft to visit Pluto, providing the first close-up images of the dwarf planet and its moons. The images revealed a diverse and geologically active surface, with features such as towering ice mountains, vast plains of nitrogen ice, and a heart-shaped region known as Tombaugh Regio. Overall, Pluto remains an object of scientific curiosity and continues to be studied by astronomers and planetary scientists.
