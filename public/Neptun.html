Neptun

Neptune is the eighth and farthest known planet from the Sun in our solar system. It is the fourth-largest planet by diameter, with a diameter of approximately 30,599 miles (49,244 kilometers) at its equator. Neptune is located beyond Uranus and is considered an ice giant, like Uranus. Neptune is primarily composed of water, methane, and ammonia ices, as well as rock and metal. Its atmosphere is mostly hydrogen and helium, with traces of methane that give it a blue color. The planet's atmosphere features faint bands and several large storms, including the Great Dark Spot and the Small Dark Spot. Neptune has at least 14 known moons, with the largest being Triton. Triton is unique among large moons in the solar system in that it orbits in the opposite direction of Neptune's rotation, and is believed to have been captured from the Kuiper Belt, a region of icy objects beyond Neptune. Neptune also has a system of rings, though they are much fainter and narrower than the rings of Saturn. Like Uranus, Neptune has only been visited by one spacecraft, the Voyager 2 mission in 1989. Voyager 2 provided the first close-up images of Neptune and its moons, as well as important data on the planet's magnetic field, atmosphere, and rings. Overall, Neptune is a fascinating and enigmatic planet, with a unique set of moons, rings, and weather patterns that continue to intrigue scientists and astronomers.
