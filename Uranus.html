Uranus

Uranus is the seventh planet from the Sun and the third-largest planet in our solar system. It has a diameter of approximately 31,518 miles (50,724 kilometers) at its equator and is located between Saturn and Neptune. Uranus is an ice giant, meaning it is primarily composed of water, methane, and ammonia ices, as well as rock and metal. It has a thick atmosphere of hydrogen and helium, with traces of methane that give it a blue-green color. Unlike Jupiter and Saturn, Uranus has no well-defined atmospheric bands or storms. Uranus has at least 27 known moons, with the largest being Titania, Oberon, Umbriel, Ariel, and Miranda. Miranda is particularly interesting, as it has an unusually varied and complex terrain that suggests geological activity in the past. Uranus also has a system of rings, though they are much fainter and less extensive than the rings of Jupiter and Saturn. Uranus is unique in the solar system for its extreme tilt, with its rotational axis tilted at an angle of 97.77 degrees relative to its orbit around the Sun. This means that the planet's poles are almost in its orbital plane, causing extreme seasonal variations in its weather patterns. Despite its scientific interest, Uranus has been visited only once by a spacecraft, the Voyager 2 mission in 1986. Voyager 2 provided the first close-up images of Uranus and its moons, as well as important data on the planet's magnetic field, atmosphere, and rings.
